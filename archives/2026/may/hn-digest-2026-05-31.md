## 5. [Restartable Sequences](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48346019) (↑55, 💬10)
**Article:** The author explains restartable sequences (rseq), a Linux 4.18+ feature that allows thread-safe data structures without locks or atomics by having the kernel update TLS memory with CPU numbers and providing abort-capable critical sections. They present benchmark results showing dramatic speedups: 3x faster on a 4-core Raspberry Pi, 34x faster on a 128-core Ampere Altra, and 43x faster on a 96-core Threadripper compared to traditional sharded mutex approaches. The article details how rseq works at the assembly level and why it overcomes cache contention and scheduling issues that plague lock-free programming on many-core systems.

**Comments:** Commenters acknowledge rseq's power, with one noting that it's essentially a light form of software transactional memory implemented at the kernel level with zero abstraction cost. Several suggest practical alternatives for testing: renting spot instances for under $10/hour instead of buying a $20,000 workstation. One commenter links to the librseq library maintained by the rseq implementer, noting that most applications don't need raw assembly.
